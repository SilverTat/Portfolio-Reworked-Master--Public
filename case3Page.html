Jump too case study

Brief / Problem space

During Designstudio - smart societies my project team and i worked with LBVA and VIVAB, two municipally sponsored companies that act as water managers for Halmstad, Laholm and Falkenbergs municipals. The brief, provided by LBVA and VIVAB, expressed a need to change user habits and attitudes towards the use of freshwater, a.k.a. drinking / tap water.

LBVA and VIVAB wanted to make actors such as, private individuals and companies conscious of freshwater as a finite resource and as a prerequisite for all life to function normally. This involved making actors stop wasting freshwater on, e.g. cleaning their cars.

LBVA and VIVAB had previously been cofounders of a project developed another design firm. The project was conducted to develop a tool to map actor's water usage, this along with a project report, was handed over to the project group for further development.

&

Goals

In short the goal of the design studio was to make actors more conscious about their fresh water usage and ultimately prevent wasteful water usage.

Results

After a lot of iterations the disgn resulted in a satisfactory visual artefact, with further room for improvement. But it would function as a concept for further development.

Serveral different service design methods were used throughout the project.

  -The first of these were a customer journey presenting how a user would navigate the service today.
  -The second journey was based on current implementation efforts and systems being used by LBVA & VIVAB.
  -The third journey was dubbed "preffered future state" and tried to present the most satisfactory journey a customer could have.

• A business model canvas was created.

• A service roadmap was created.

• Two artefacts, one for the web and one for mobile usage.

• Finally general guidelines for further development.

Takeaways - Summary

Tunnel vision - Breaking free from tunnel vision can be hard once the design process is already started.

Service design methods - Service deisn methods are often very complex and hard to understand. But they can be immensly usefull when designing and explaining a concept!

Project leading - Leading a smaller subgroup focused, mostly, on the service design of the project gave me further appreciation for project leading. Having a constant overview of what is being done and if deadlines are being met can be stressfull. But if done right is an adequatley challenging and fun task!

Takeaways

During this design studio I learned how hard it can be to get out of tunnel vision focus once the design process has already started. The project group initially wanted to design a service based on current measures being taken by LBVA and VIVAB, however this did not fulfil the course goals and it was hard to shift focus.

I also learned a lot more about service design and what methods might be suited to which parts of a design process.

I gained further knowledge in project managing within a smaller subgroup of a project and how to properly communicate relevant and important topics of discussion with other subgroups within a larger project.

Research

The project team started off the initial research by trying to understand the concept of “water smartness” that was mentioned in the brief supplied by LBVA and VIVAB. However, there was no generally agreed upon or accepted definition of water smartness established. The group came to the conclusion that this is most likely due to lacking interest and the subject not being addressed properly.

The project team decided to conduct a omvärldsanalys to explore other countries perspective on water and how they handle their freshwater supplies. Different initiatives from countries to use their freshwater supplies in a sustainable way were studied, as well as academic literature and other information channels across the web. The project report from COWI supplied by LBVA and VIVAB was also used during the research.

During the research the project group observed that small and medium companies said they were willing to become more conscious of their freshwater users, and in turn, become more watersmart but that they did not know how to. We also observed, what we defined as, a conscious or unconscious ignorance within companies regarding “water smartness”.

Finally we observed that most of the information pertaining to “watersmartness” were found in academic articles / literature and global agendas. Further most measures were designed for private households. In short we observed a plethora of measures that private households could take to become more “watersmart” but also observed a lack of measures designed for companies.

To garner further insights of the water consumption and water consciousness of companies the project team developed an interview guide. Through the interviews that were conducted we concluded that most companies had a clear overview of how the companies used their water, but they couldn't answer questions regarding how much water specific parts of their facilities consumed. When the companies were questioned about if the had taken any measures to become more “watersmart” the companies mentioned more general measures like water-saving faucets, and nothing company specific.

Insights from the research

What is water smartness? - No clear singular definition.

Conscious or unconscious ignorance - Small and medium companies are willing to use freshwater more responsibly, but dont know how to.

Softcore gatekeeping - Most of the publically available information regarding, water smartness, is available in academic litterature and global agendas making it hard to find.

Differences in available measures - General measures that could be taken to become more watersmart were plentifull. Mainly by switching to watersmart faucets, showerheads and etc. Measures for different industrial endeavours were nowhere to be found.

Ideation

Initially the group had problems actually starting the ideation phase. Given that this design studio was a course during our studies at Halmstad University we had certain criteria the project had to fulfil. This meant that the initial idea the group had, developing a service that implemented current efforts and implementation from LBVA and VIVAB side, didn't fully meet the course goals. Therefore the group decided to shift focus and look even further into the future.

We divided the project group into two different subgroups, one that would develop the artefact that was primarily designed for the users of the water (not LBVA and VIVAB) and another one that would develop the service with tools created for service design. Each group also had a primary head / leader that would act as a project manager within the subgroups. I was appointed as head of the service design group and a colleague was appointed as the head of the other.

Both groups had daily scrums to keep eachother updated on what they were working on and how far into the process each group had gotten. During these scrums both subgroups also checked that the groups were taking, either the service design part or the artefact design part of the project in the direction the respective groups had in mind.

This lead to the development of three different customer journeys, one for the current state, future state and one called preferred future state. The current state customer journey was based on existing artefacts and communication between LBVA and VIVAB and different actors. The future state customer journey was based on measures that are currently being implemented and what could be done to further their usefulness in making actors more conscious of their freshwater usage. And finally the preferred future state customer journey was created visualise a, for the project group, ideal customer journey.

A scenario was then created based on the preferred future state customer journey, as a sort of short novel. The scenario had the purpose of explaining the customer journey more broadly and pedagogically.

A service blueprint could then enter development based on the customer journey and the scenario. The service blueprint was meant to give LBVA and VIVAB to a clear overview how the technical parts of the service would work together with employees developing company specific measures.

A business model canvas was the developed to concretely explain to LBVA and VIVAB how the service would deliver value to them, consumers of freshwater and society as whole, framed from a climate perspective.

A service roadmap was also developed to help LBVA and VIVAB start development and implementation of the service, if they so wished. A service specification was also developed to give a high fidelity explanation of what and how different parts of the service roadmap were to be developed and implemented. As well as explaining more in depth what the different “sprints” in the service roadmap entailed and why they were important.

Prototype & Evaluation

The project group started working on a visual artefact that actors would interact with when using the service. Before prototyping of the UI could begin the project group started of by doing more research about how similar artefacts was designed around the world. A particularly relevant area of study was energy consumption and how this was presented in a user friendly way. We quickly realised that energy consumption was often presented in graphs and different energy curves.

With this in mind the part of the project group responsible for the visual artefact initially created an artefact for the service. The artefact contained various visualisations of a company's freshwater usage in the form of 2D graphs.

However due to me mainly being responsible for the subgroup developing the service my group was not overly involved in this process. As previously mentioned.

During critique sessions with other student colleagues the artefact was refined further. As an example we garnered insights about how to present water consumption efficiently. Due to water often being measured in three dimensions and not two dimensions the project group decided to use 3D graphs for representation of the water usage instead of 2D graphs.

The initial information architecture was also changed due to the artefact being perceived as overwhelming or boring. The project group also performed a type of cardsorting to explore how users would want their water usage presented to them in this type of artefact. In the cardsorting participants had to sort out predetermined elements that would be part of the artefact, however they see fit.

The cardsorting resulted in further insights of how the informational architecture should be designed within the artefact.

Final artefact

The designstudio resulted in a plethora of service design tools for development and implementation of the service.

First and foremost was three different customer journey maps, one was developed based on the current service. The second customer journey was based on efforts currently being implemented and how these efforts could potentially affect actors and help them reduce their wasteful use of the finite freshwater supply. Third and lastly, we create a “Preferred future state customer journey” this customer journey envisioned a future not limited by current efforts already being implemented.

Secondly a service blueprint was created to envision how the service would work between all involved actors and artefacts. This service blueprint was based of the “preferred future state customer journey”. The service presented what actions customers would do and how this corresponded to fronstage actions (the employees at LBVA and VIVAB), how the customers would interact with the artefacts, how these interactions were then communicated to the backstage actions taken by the employees at LBVA and VIVAB and finally backstage support processes handled by the databases of these artefacts.

Thirdly business model canvas was drafted to clearly convey how the service, based on the “preferred future state map” would create value for LBVA and VIVAB, private actors and society as a whole.

Fourth a service roadmap for development and implementation of the service was developed to help LBVA and VIVAB plan a potential development and implementation strategy for the service. The service roadmap spanned a total of eight years but efforts to implement such a large scale service could potentially be longer due to the sheer logistics. The service roadmap had three major pillars of development and implementation, these are: Digital infrastructure (pink/purple), Physical infrastructure (Blue) and Marketing (green).

Fifth, the project team developed two artefacts for the service concept. One was developed for the web, with PC’s and laptops in mind. The other was developed as a mobile application. The project group decided that the artefact developed for the web would present more in depth data regarding water consumption due to the, generally, larger screen real estate available on PC’s and laptops. Meanwhile the app, designed with phones in mind, would present the most important data, but less data as a whole.

Finally the project group drafted some general guidelines for further development of the service, this encompassed further development of the web and app artefact. As Well as certificates that would need development. These certificates was something LBVA and VIVAB had expressed that they wanted to be able to supply companies who were “water smart” with.

The delivered conceptual service solution had companies of varying sizes in mind and not private individuals / households. This was due to the project group observing that a plethora of measures were available to households to become more “watersmart”, as well as companies of varying sizes being the biggest consumers freshwater.
