CASE BRIEF

During Designstudio - smart societies my project team and i worked with LBVA and VIVAB, two municipally sponsored companies that act as water managers for Halmstad, Laholm and Falkenbergs municipals. The brief, provided by LBVA and VIVAB, expressed a need to change user habits and attitudes towards the use of freshwater, a.k.a. drinking / tap water.

LBVA and VIVAB wanted to make actors such as, private individuals and companies conscious of freshwater as a finite resource and as a prerequisite for all life to function normally. This involved making actors stop wasting freshwater on, e.g. cleaning their cars.

LBVA and VIVAB had previously co-founded a another project. The goal of the project was to develop a tool to map actor's water usage. This, along with a project report, was handed over to us at the begining at the project for further development.

RESULTS

After a lot of iterations the design resulted in a satisfactory visual artefact, with further room for improvement. But it would function as a concept for further development.

Serveral different service design methods were used throughout the project. Three customer journeys were developed to represent different stages of implementation. The first of these were a customer journey presenting how a user would navigate the service today. The second journey was based on future implementation efforts and systems planned by LBVA & VIVAB. The third journey was dubbed "preffered future state" and tried to present the most satisfactory journey a customer could have.

The final solution consisted of:

• A BUSINESS MODEL CANVAS.

• A SERVICE ROADMAP.

• TWO ARTEFACTS, ONE FOR WEB AND ONE FOR MOBILE USAGE.

• GENERAL GUIDELINES FOR FURTHER DEVELOPMENT.

RESEARCH

The project team started off the initial research by trying to understand the concept of “water smartness” that was mentioned in the brief supplied by LBVA and VIVAB. However, there was no generally agreed upon or accepted definition of water smartness established. The group came to the conclusion that this is most likely due to lacking interest and the subject not being addressed properly.

The project team decided to conduct a omvärldsanalys to explore other countries perspectives on water and how they handle their freshwater supplies. Thus we studdied different initiatives from other countries and how they their freshwater supplies in a sustainable way. Academic literature and other information channels across the web were used. The project report from COWI supplied by LBVA and VIVAB was also used for this research.

During the research the project group observed that small and medium companies said they were willing to become more conscious of their freshwater usage, and in turn, become more watersmart but that they did not know how to. We also observed, what we defined as, a conscious or unconscious ignorance within companies regarding “water smartness”.

Finally we observed that most of the information pertaining to “watersmartness” were found in academic articles / literature and global agendas. Further most measures were designed for private households. In short we observed a plethora of measures that private households could take to become more “watersmart” but also observed a lack of measures designed for companies.

To garner further insights of the water consumption and water consciousness of companies the project team developed an interview guide. Through the interviews we concluded that most companies had a clear overview of how the companies used their water, but they couldn't answer questions regarding how much water specific parts of their facilities consumed. When the companies were questioned about if the had taken any measures to become more “watersmart” the companies mentioned more general measures like water-saving faucets, but nothing company specific.

IDEATION

We divided the project group into two different teams, one that would develop the artefact that was primarily designed for the users of the water and not LBVA and VIVAB and another one that would develop the service behind the artefact. Each group also had a member that would act as a project manager within the teams. I was appointed as head of the service design group.

Both teams had daily scrums to keep eachother updated on what they were working on and how far into the process each group had gotten. These scrums also served to make sure that the direction either team was taking was in line with what the other team had in mind.

This lead to the development of three different customer journeys, one for the current state, future state and one called preferred future state. The current state customer journey was based on existing artefacts already implemented by LBVA and VIVAB and different actors. The future state customer journey was based on measures that are currently being implemented and what could be done to further their usefulness in making actors more conscious of their freshwater usage. And finally the preferred future state customer journey was created to visualise an ideal customer journey.

A scenario was then created based on the preferred future state customer journey, as a sort of short novel. The scenario had the purpose of explaining the customer journey in depth and more pedagogically.

A service blueprint could then enter development based on the customer journey and the scenario. The service blueprint was meant to give LBVA and VIVAB a clear overview of how the technical parts of the service would work together with employees developing company specific measures.

A business model canvas was the developed to concretely explain to LBVA and VIVAB how the service would deliver value to them, consumers of freshwater and society as whole, framed from a climate perspective.

A service roadmap was also developed to help LBVA and VIVAB start development and implementation of the service.

A service specification was also developed to give a high fidelity explanation of what and how different parts of the service roadmap were to be developed and implemented. As well as explaining more in depth what the different “sprints” in the service roadmap entailed and why they were important.

PROTOTYPING

A high fidelity prototype based on the final frankensteined wireframe was then created in figma. This prototype served to show the navigational architecture, and a booking system in high fidelity. And was ultimately meant as a highly detailed example of how a future implementation would look and work in practice.

Besides the figma prototype a coded prototype was created by me; in react native. The prototype was of mixed fidelity. Initially I expected it'd be easy to create a calendar, but I ran into problems when trying to make it dynamic. A variety of different prebuilt calendars were tested before settling on an adequate one. The coded prototype was created solely for the purpose of showing that it was technically possible to create a booking system, and was far from as complete as the figma prototype, and only a proof of concept.

EVALUATION

The project team developed an evaluation guide for the Figma prototype, focusing on task completion time and follow-up questions about the prototype's navigational and informational architecture. Four evaluations were conducted: two with non-expert users and two with expert users of the current system. These interviews yielded minor insights into improvements, such as clarifying how to display the selected day and the current day. However, the limited sample size likely restricted the amount of feedback gathered.

Most major issues were addressed in the early development stages through critique sessions with other students during the design studio. These sessions highlighted areas for improvement, particularly in presenting information more clearly, such as specifying the region a shift pertained to and enhancing button readability.

The evaluation process led to significant refinements in the final artefact, including a streamlined menu structure, a clearer schedule layout, larger buttons for easier interaction, and updated schedule markers. For instance, marking the current date was a topic of extensive debate, influenced by variations in how other scheduling applications handle this feature. Ultimately, the team settled on a marking approach that balanced functionality and user preferences.
